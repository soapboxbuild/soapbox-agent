Glossary

BREEAM (Building Research Establishment Environmental Assessment Method) is a sustainability assessment method that measures the sustainable value of infrastructure and buildings using a series of categories, ranging from energy to ecology. Each of these categories addresses the most influential factors, including low-impact design and carbon emissions reduction, design durability and resilience, adaption to climate change; and ecological value and biodiversity protection. The BREEAM ratings range from Acceptable (In-Use scheme only) to Pass, Good, Very Good, Excellent to Outstanding and it is reflected in a series of stars on the BREEAM certificate. BREEAM is not currently developed for residential properties.
Energy Rating indicates the percentage of operating properties with an ENERGY STAR score, EPC rating, or any other energy rating out of the total number of operating properties in the investment portfolio. ENERGY STAR is a joint program run by the US Department of Energy and EPA to help identify energy-efficient products, benchmark energy, water, and waste data, and assess building energy performance. The label identifies top-performing, cost-effective products and properties, through a series of set specifications, testing procedures, and verifications. EPC (Energy Performance Certificate) is a four-page document that sets out the energy efficiency of a property on a traffic light system of A to G, with A being the most efficient. An EPC provides an indication of how much it will cost to heat and power a property. A renewable energy certificate (REC) is a market-based instrument that represents the property rights to the environmental, social, and other non-power attributes of renewable electricity generation. RECs are issued when one megawatt-hour (MWh) of electricity is generated and delivered to the electricity grid from a renewable energy resource.
Green Certifications measure the number of operating properties with recognized building certifications such as LEED, BREEAM, and/or similar certifications out of the total number of operating properties in the investment portfolio.
GRESB (Global Real Estate Sustainability Benchmark) is an international organization established in 2009. GRESB provides standards and benchmarks to track environmental, social, and governance (ESG) performance for real estate and infrastructure investments. Institutional and financial investors use GRESB to monitor investments and understand ESG opportunities and risks.
LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is a third-party green building certification program and a globally recognized sustainability standard for the design, construction, and operation of highly efficient green buildings. The LEED rating systems are made up of prerequisites and credits. Prerequisites are required elements or green building strategies that must be included in any LEED-certified project. Credits are optional elements that projects can pursue to gain points toward LEED certification. The levels of certification are then awarded based on points and follow the following thresholds: Certified (40–49 points), Silver (50–59 points), Gold (60–79 points) and Platinum (80+ points).
Net Zero refers to a state in which the greenhouse gases going into the atmosphere are balanced by removal from the atmosphere.
Physical Risk refers to financial risks arising from the physical effects of climate change. This includes the damages and losses to property, infrastructure, supply chains, and the built environment caused by acute or chronic climate events.
CRREM (Carbon Risk Real Estate Monitor) Stranding Year is the year when the asset's GHG intensity is higher than CRREM 1.5°C decarbonization pathway. Stranded assets are properties that will not meet future energy efficiency standards and market expectations and might be increasingly exposed to the risk of early economic obsolescence.
Transition Risk refers to the risk of losing or gaining value due to the changes in policy, regulation, technology and consumer preferences that are needed to shift to a low-carbon economy and mitigate the physical impacts of climate change. This risk may affect the performance and valuation of firms, investments, and sectors that rely on carbon-intensive activities or technologies.

Endnotes

Asset Class Groups have been developed to allow for internal benchmarking against similar investments in the same or similar asset class. The asset class groups established are updated annually and kept as consistent as possible. Sponsors were assigned asset classes based on the asset class that is covered by the majority of their sq ft. Groups include Industrial / Logistics / Self Storage, Multifamily / Senior Housing, Office, Retail, Data Centers, Life Science, SFR, Cold Storage, and Marinas.
Green Street Sector Risk Rating is scored "Low", "Below Average", "Average", "Above Average" and "High" depending on the sector regulations (30%), energy/emissions intensity (50%), compliance costs (10%), grid "greenness" (10%). Metrics are pulled from the Green Street Sector "E" Sensitivity score list.
CRREM (Carbon Risk Real Estate Monitor) Stranding Year is the year when the asset's GHG intensity is higher than CRREM 1.5°C decarbonization pathway. Stranded assets are properties that will not meet future energy efficiency standards and market expectations and might be increasingly exposed to the risk of early economic obsolescence. The stranding year is the first stranding year projected; it is not representative of full portfolio stranding.
Physical Impact are risks resulting from climate change such as droughts, floods, extreme precipitation, wildfires, heat stress, water stress, and hurricanes, among others. It is scored "Low", "Medium" or "High" depending on the severity of the climate event risk.
% Sq ft w/ Green Building Certifications and % Sq ft w/ Energy Ratings are based on the total sq ft with one or more Green Building Certification or Energy Rating (as applicable) available divided by total sq ft of standing assets. Metrics that are "Not Available" mean the investment is under development or non-operating at the time of this report. Metrics that are "Not Applicable" mean that due to the nature of the asset type this data will not be collected.
Scorecard Response Rates are calculated at Fund and MIR level to understand how representative the scorecard averages are of investments in the fund.
GRESB Reporting and Net Zero Policy metrics are based on annual ESG Questionnaire responses provided by sponsors, representing ESG initiatives in place for the reporting year. Sponsor response options were 'Yes', 'No' and 'In Progress'. Percentages are based on the total number of sponsors with a Net Zero Policy in place or in progress / GRESB Reporting or alignment divided by the total number of sponsors in the fund / asset class / MIR.
Energy Data Coverage by area indicates the sponsor's energy data area covered divided by the gross floor area. Metrics that are "Not Available" mean the investment is under development or non-operating at the time of this report.
Renewable Energy metric represents the percentage of renewable energy generated on-site and consumed by landlord and/or off-site renewable energy procured by landlord, as a percentage of total energy consumed by the site. Metrics that are "Not Available" mean the investment is under development or non-operating at the time of this report.
